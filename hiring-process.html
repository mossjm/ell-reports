1. Executive Summary

Elm Lake Cranberry faces a challenge shared by agricultural operations across Wisconsin: finding and retaining qualified farm workers in an increasingly competitive labor market. With approximately six employees managing 150 acres of cranberry production, every hire matters ‚Äî a single bad fit or unexpected departure can significantly disrupt operations.

This report provides a comprehensive, actionable hiring improvement plan tailored to Elm Lake Cranberry’s specific situation. It covers the Wisconsin agricultural labor market landscape, pay benchmarks grounded in current USDA and state data, a detailed catalog of recruiting channels with specific contacts, an honest assessment of the H-2A visa program’s viability for a small operation, AI-assisted hiring tools, ready-to-post job descriptions, a retention strategy built around completion bonuses and career progression, and a 90-day action plan to put it all into motion.

Key findings: - The Lake Region (WI/MI/MN) average farm wage reached $20.92/hr in April 2025, up $1.75 from the prior year. Elm Lake must pay at or above this level for skilled positions to remain competitive. - Mid-State Technical College (MSTC) in Wisconsin Rapids is the single highest-value recruiting channel ‚Äî their Agriculture programs (Farm Operation, Agribusiness Science & Technology, Ag Diesel Engines) produce graduates with exactly the skills ELC needs. - H-2A is not cost-effective for an operation of this size. The math works against you below ~10 workers. Alternatives exist (labor-sharing cooperatives, H-2B for non-ag seasonal tasks). - A structured retention bonus program ($1.50‚Äì$2.00/hr banked and paid at season completion) combined with a clear skills progression ladder can dramatically reduce turnover. - Three ready-to-post job descriptions are included ‚Äî John can literally post these tomorrow.

2. Wisconsin Agricultural Labor Market Analysis

2.1 Current Labor Shortage Data

Wisconsin’s agricultural labor market is historically tight. Key data points:

  • 52,000 workers were hired directly by farms in the Lake Region (MI, MN, WI) during the April 2025 reference week (USDA NASS). This is down from 62,000 in July 2024 and 65,000 in October 2024, reflecting seasonal fluctuation but also a broader contraction.
  • Wisconsin farm employment is estimated at roughly 24,000 employees statewide (UW-Extension lower-bound estimate based on UI-covered workers; actual number including small farms is higher).
  • The unemployment rate in Wood County has remained below 4% consistently, meaning there is effectively no surplus labor pool waiting to be hired.
  • The USDA Economic Research Service reports that wages for nonsupervisory farm occupations ranged from $17.23/hr (general farmworkers) to $19.07/hr (equipment operators) nationally in 2024, with year-over-year increases exceeding 3% in five of six occupation categories.

2.2 Competing Industries Pulling Workers

Farm workers in central Wisconsin are being recruited away by industries that offer:

Industry Typical Wage Key Appeal
Amazon / Warehousing $18‚Äì$22/hr Climate-controlled, consistent hours, benefits
Construction $20‚Äì$30/hr Higher hourly, seasonal overlap with farming
Manufacturing (paper, plastics) $18‚Äì$25/hr Year-round stability, benefits packages
Kwik Trip / Retail $15‚Äì$18/hr + benefits Low physical demand, advancement paths
Dairy Operations $16‚Äì$20/hr + housing Year-round work, often includes housing

The core problem: Entry-level farm work competes directly with Amazon warehouses (Plover, ~25 miles away), Verso/Domtar paper operations, and construction trades ‚Äî all of which offer comparable or better pay with more predictable schedules and climate-controlled environments.

2.3 Seasonal vs. Year-Round Labor Dynamics for Cranberry

Cranberry farming has a distinct labor cycle:

  • Year-round (core crew, 3‚Äì4 employees): Bog maintenance, frost protection, irrigation management, equipment maintenance, dike and ditch work. These are the essential positions requiring the most skill and institutional knowledge.
  • Spring (April‚ÄìJune): Frost season ramps up labor needs. Overnight frost watches, pump operation, weed management begin.
  • Peak season (September‚ÄìNovember): Harvest is the most labor-intensive period. Requires operators for harvesters, berry pumps, trucks, and intake facilities. Typically need 2‚Äì4 additional seasonal workers.
  • Winter (December‚ÄìMarch): Reduced crew. Ice sanding, equipment repair, pruning, flood management. Some operations run with as few as 1‚Äì2 employees.

Implication for ELC: You need a dual-track hiring strategy ‚Äî (1) recruit and retain 4‚Äì5 year-round skilled workers, and (2) develop a reliable pipeline for 2‚Äì4 seasonal harvest workers.

2.4 Demographic Trends

  • Wisconsin’s rural population continues to age. The median age in Wood County is approximately 42 years, above the state average.
  • Younger workers (18‚Äì35) are migrating to Madison, Milwaukee, and Twin Cities metro areas.
  • Hispanic/Latino workers make up an increasing share of Wisconsin’s agricultural workforce (estimated 40‚Äì50% of seasonal hired farm labor statewide), though this proportion is lower in central Wisconsin compared to the southeastern part of the state.
  • The number of farms in Wood County has declined steadily ‚Äî fewer farms means fewer people growing up with agricultural skills and affinity.

3.3 Benefits That Matter

For a small operation that can’t match corporate benefit packages, focus on what farm workers actually value:

  1. Housing assistance ‚Äî Even a stipend ($200‚Äì$400/mo) or helping connect workers with affordable local housing is huge. If ELC has or could add any on-farm housing, this becomes the #1 recruiting advantage.
  2. End-of-season bonus ‚Äî See Section 8. This is the single most effective retention tool.
  3. Flexible scheduling ‚Äî “Work hard when it’s time to work, go home when we’re done” beats a rigid clock.
  4. Equipment/gear provided ‚Äî Boots, gloves, rain gear, sun protection. Small cost, big signal that you invest in your people.
  5. Health insurance contribution ‚Äî Even $200‚Äì$300/mo toward an ACA plan stands out. Most small farms offer nothing.
  6. Fuel / transportation help ‚Äî Gas prices hit rural workers hard. A fuel card or mileage supplement is valued highly.
  7. Paid time for training/certifications ‚Äî CDL prep, pesticide applicator license, etc. Invest in them and they stay.

3.4 Competitor Landscape

  • Other cranberry operations (Wetherby, Glacial Lake, Habelman Brothers): Comparable pay ranges; larger operations may offer better benefits packages but less autonomy and variety.
  • Dairy farms (Marathon/Portage County): $16‚Äì$20/hr, often include housing. Year-round work is appealing to many.
  • Potato operations (Plover/Bancroft area): Seasonal peaks overlap with cranberry harvest. Pay $16‚Äì$20/hr for seasonal, $18‚Äì$22 for CDL/equipment operators.
  • Vegetable/nursery operations: Similar seasonal dynamics, generally lower pay ($14‚Äì$17/hr).

5. H-2A Visa Program Analysis

5.1 Program Overview

The H-2A Temporary Agricultural Workers program allows U.S. employers to bring foreign nationals to the U.S. to fill temporary agricultural jobs when domestic workers are not available. It is administered by three federal agencies: Department of Labor (DOL), USCIS, and the Department of State.

5.2 Requirements

  1. Temporary or seasonal need ‚Äî Must demonstrate the job is temporary in nature
  2. Domestic recruitment effort ‚Äî Must actively recruit U.S. workers first and show insufficient response
  3. No adverse effect on U.S. workers ‚Äî Cannot displace domestic workers
  4. Wage requirements ‚Äî Must pay the Adverse Effect Wage Rate (AEWR), prevailing wage, agreed-upon collective bargaining wage, or federal/state minimum wage ‚Äî whichever is highest
  5. Wisconsin 2025 AEWR: $18.15/hr (Lake Region ‚Äî MI, WI, MN; this actually decreased 1.9% from $18.50 in 2024)
  6. Housing ‚Äî Must provide free, government-inspected housing for all H-2A workers (and any domestic workers who cannot reasonably return home daily)
  7. Transportation ‚Äî Must provide or reimburse inbound/outbound transportation from workers’ home country and daily transportation to/from the worksite
  8. Meals ‚Äî Must provide three meals a day or furnish free and convenient cooking facilities
  9. Three-fourths guarantee ‚Äî Must guarantee employment for at least 75% of the contract period’s work hours
  10. Tools and equipment ‚Äî Must provide all tools, supplies, and equipment at no cost

5.5 Viability Assessment: Is H-2A Right for ELC?

Verdict: Almost certainly not, at current scale.

  • Housing is the dealbreaker. If ELC doesn’t have compliant worker housing, the cost of renting or building it for just 3 workers is prohibitive ($27,000‚Äì$39,000 for a single season).
  • The all-in cost per worker ($38‚Äì$49/hr) is roughly double what you’d pay a domestic worker with a generous retention bonus.
  • Administrative burden is significant ‚Äî DOL audits, housing inspections, record-keeping requirements are designed for operations bringing in 20+ workers.
  • The 75-day lead time makes it inflexible if your needs change.

5.6 Alternatives

H-2B (Non-Agricultural Temporary Workers): - Could work for ancillary tasks (warehouse, processing facility work) that don’t qualify as “agricultural” - Subject to annual cap (66,000 visas nationally), much harder to get - Not recommended for ELC’s core farm labor needs

Pooling with Other Growers: - Several Wisconsin cranberry growers could jointly sponsor H-2A workers through a farm labor contractor or joint employer petition - Workers rotate between operations based on harvest timing - Reduces per-farm cost significantly (shared housing, shared admin) - Contact: Wisconsin State Cranberry Growers Association (WSCGA) to explore interest ‚Äî (715) 423-2070, wiscran.org

H-2A Labor Contractors: - Companies that hold H-2A certifications and “rent” crews to farms - You pay a higher hourly rate ($25‚Äì$35/hr) but avoid all housing, transportation, and administrative burden - Google “H-2A labor contractor Wisconsin” or ask WSCGA for referrals

8. Retention Strategy

8.1 End-of-Season Completion Bonus Structure

The single most effective retention tool for seasonal and year-round farm workers. Structure it so the payout is meaningful and the vesting is clear.

Proposed Structure:

Worker Type Bonus Mechanism Example Payout
Year-round employees $1.50/hr banked, paid annually on Dec 15 ~$3,120/yr (40hrs √ó 52wks √ó $1.50)
Seasonal harvest (Sept‚ÄìNov) $2.00/hr banked, paid at season completion ~$1,040 (40hrs √ó 13wks √ó $2.00)
Equipment operators $1.50/hr banked + $500 harvest completion bonus ~$3,620/yr

Rules: - Bonus accrues every hour worked but is only paid if the employee completes the season/year in good standing - “Good standing” = didn’t quit, wasn’t fired for cause, maintained acceptable attendance - Paid as a lump sum ‚Äî the psychological impact of a single check for $1,000‚Äì$3,000 is much greater than the equivalent spread across paychecks - Make the bonus structure explicit at hiring ‚Äî it’s a recruiting tool as much as a retention tool

8.3 Team Culture Recommendations

  1. Weekly team breakfast or lunch (during season) ‚Äî 30 minutes, $50‚Äì$75 in food. Builds camaraderie and gives people a reason to look forward to coming in.
  2. End-of-harvest celebration ‚Äî BBQ, bonfire, whatever fits the culture. Recognize contributions publicly. Hand out bonus checks here for maximum impact.
  3. “Gil’s Lead” structure ‚Äî Leverage Gil’s strength as farm manager. Empower him to mentor and develop workers, not just direct them. People quit bosses, not jobs ‚Äî Gil being a great manager is ELC’s secret weapon.
  4. Safety culture ‚Äî Take it seriously and visibly. Workers who feel safe stay longer. Brief daily safety check-ins, proper PPE provided (not just available), no tolerance for unsafe shortcuts.
  5. Input and ownership ‚Äî Ask workers for ideas. “How could we do this better?” People who feel heard stay engaged. If someone suggests a process improvement and you implement it, give them credit publicly.
  6. Seasonal rituals ‚Äî Cranberry farming has natural milestones (first flood, frost season start, first harvest day, last truck). Mark them. These become the traditions that build loyalty.

9.3 Interview Questions

Use these 10 questions, adapted to the specific role:

  1. “Tell me about a time you had to work in tough weather or uncomfortable conditions. How’d you handle it?” ‚Äî Tests grit and honesty.
  2. “What equipment have you operated? Walk me through a typical day at your last job.” ‚Äî Reveals actual experience level (vs. resume inflation).
  3. “What would you do if a piece of equipment broke down in the field and Gil wasn’t around?” ‚Äî Tests problem-solving and mechanical aptitude.
  4. “Harvest season means long days and weekend work for about 6‚Äì8 weeks. How does that fit with your life?” ‚Äî Uncovers schedule conflicts before they become no-shows.
  5. “Why are you interested in farm work specifically, rather than another job?” ‚Äî Tests genuine interest vs. “I’ll take anything.”
  6. “Tell me about a time you had to work closely with a small team. What was your role?” ‚Äî Small crew dynamics matter enormously.
  7. “What’s your transportation situation? How far is the drive?” ‚Äî Long commutes are the #1 predictor of early turnover in rural jobs.
  8. “How do you feel about learning new technology? We use GPS-guided equipment and automated systems.” ‚Äî Tests willingness to learn and adapt.
  9. “What would make this the kind of job you’d want to stick with for a few years?” ‚Äî Reveals what they value. Lets you address it.
  10. “Do you have any certifications ‚Äî CDL, pesticide applicator, first aid? Would you be interested in getting any?” ‚Äî Identifies growth-oriented candidates.
